\subsection{Ordered External Electric Fields as catalysts}
Chemical reactivity can be broadly reduced down to the dynamics of electrons, it stands to reason that being able to utilise electrostatics to manipulate the electrons that govern reactivity is a grand challenge of chemistry. This notion has been at the forefront of chemical research for as long as electron directing groups have been utilised to manipulate the strength of bonds, however the idea of influencing a reaction from a distance using electrostatics has only been explored in the past century.\\

The methods to this approach were initially quite crude, involving the use of homogenous ionic species\cite{Pocker1970}, though through the use of early \ai techniques, the methods were able to be more rigorously and precisely investigated. These studies in the early 90s sought to understand solvent effects and more generally the effects of, localised electric fields (EF) on chemical reactions, such as the Friedel-Crafts\cite{Carbonell1991} and Menshutkin\cite{Sola1991} reactions. Further evidence for the direct influence of electrostatic catalysis came from biology, as the idea that within an enzyme, an internal electric field (IEF) generated by charged amino acids within the binding site could effectively be creating a pre-organised environment that would stabilise a substrate's transition state (TS)\cite{Warshel2006}.\\

Whilst developing the valence bond model of chemical reactivity, \citea{Shaik1981} recognised that the definitions of chemical bonding that emerged from the theory indicated that all bonds, irrespective of the nature of their component atoms could be described in a polar context. This inspired \citea{Sini1991} to explore the idea, developing a model of bonding that described all bonds as a superposition of a spin paired, purely covalent interaction and two possible ionic interactions, as shown in \ref{eqn:ResonatingBonds}\cite{Shaik2020}.\\
\begin{gather}
\Psi_{A-B}=C_1(\ce{A-B})+C_2(\ce{A- B+})+C_3(\ce{A+B-})\label{eqn:ResonatingBonds}
\end{gather}
These theoretical formalisms laid the groundwork for further computational research into the field, with \citeauthor{Shaik2004} continuing to explore the effects of external electric fields (EEF) to first, create selectivity between competing nonpolar epoxidation and hydroxylation reactions\cite{Shaik2004} and subsequently for \citea{Meir2010} to identify the endo/exo selectivity of Diels-Alder reactions, afforded by the use of oriented EEFs (OEEFs). This theoretical exploration was put to the test experimentally by \citea{Aragones2016}, who used an OEFF directed from a scanning tunnelling microscope plate to the tip, to not only control the endo/exo selectivity of the reaction, but also to quantify and correlate the effect of the EF strength.\\

With sufficient experimental evidence and theoretical understanding of the effect of OEFFs, \citeauthor{Shaik2020} formalised the "governing effects and rules for electric-field effects on chemical reacivity"\cite{Shaik2020}, briefly summarised as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
	\item An OEEF that is oriented along a "bond axis" will polarise the bond, favouring one of the ionic species (\ref{eqn:ResonatingBonds}), eventually cleaving it heterolytically. As this polarisation occurs, the bond dipole increases, causing it to interact more strongly with the OEEF, stabilising the species in the process.
	\item Every reaction possesses a "reaction axis" which follows the flow of electrons from reactant to product, through the TS.
	\item Applying an OEEF along the "reaction axis" will stabilise the TS by increasing the bond ionicity (as per rule (a)) and, symmetry of the HOMO-LUMO overlap of the transition species allowing, will encourage mixing of the charge transfer states.
	\item A molecule with a dipole moment or that is polarisable, will rotate to align with a sufficiently strong OEFF.
\end{enumerate}
