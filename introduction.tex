\subsection{Ordered External Electric Fields as catalysts}
Chemical reactivity as a field of study can be broadly reduced down to the dynamics of electrons, it therefore goes without saying that being able to manipulate electrostatics to control reactivity is a grand challenge of chemistry. This notion has been at the forefront of chemical research for as long as electron directing groups have been utilised to manipulate the strength of bonds, however the idea of influencing a reaction from a distance using electrostatics has only been explored in the past century.

The methods to this approach were initially quite crude, involving ionic aggregates in solution to polarise reactant species\cite{Pocker1970}, though through the use of early \ai methods were able to be more specifically investigated. These later studies in the early 90s sought to understand solvent effects and more generally the effect of, localised electric fields\cite{Carbonell1991}, and as a result, a more concrete understanding of the mechanism of bond polarisation was identified\cite{Sini1991}, forming the foundations of the study of electric field catalysis.
\begin{gather}
\Psi_{A-B}=C_1(\ce{A-B})+C_2(\ce{A- B+})+C_3(\ce{A+B-})\label{eqn:ResonatingBonds}
\end{gather}
Following the work of \citea{Sini1991} who described the electron pair bond as the superposition of their spin paired covalent and two possible zwitterionic forms (as shown in \ref{eqn:ResonatingBonds}), and many subsequent studies that ill be outlined later in this report, \citeauthor{Shaik2020} formalised the "governing effects and rules for electric-field effects on chemical reacivity"\cite{Shaik2020}. Briefly summarised, the rules are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
	\item An ordered external electric field (OEEF) that is oriented along a "bond axis" will polarise the bond, favouring one of the ionic species (\ref{eqn:ResonatingBonds}), eventually cleaving it heterolytically. As this polarisation occurs, the bond dipole increases, causing it to interact more strongly with the OEEF, stabilising the species in the process.
	\item Every reaction possesses a "reaction axis" which follows the flow of electrons from reactant to product, through the transition state (TS).
	\item Applying an OEEF along the "reaction axis" will stabilise the TS by increasing the bond ionicity (as per rule Shaik (a)) and, (symmetry allowing) \red{finish this off}
	\item molecule will align with OEEF
\end{enumerate}
